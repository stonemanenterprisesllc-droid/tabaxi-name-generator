How Tabaxi names work

Tabaxi — the curious cat-folk of Dungeons & Dragons — don't carry ordinary first and last names. Each tabaxi has a single descriptive name, a short poetic phrase tied to their birth, a deed, or an omen: Cloud on the Mountaintop, Jade Shoe, Five-Timbers, Left-Handed Hummingbird. Because those names are a mouthful, tabaxi go by a nickname — usually one word plucked from the full name (Cloud, Jade). Tabaxi also belong to a clan with its own evocative name, like Distant Rain or Children of the Winter Moon.

Using these names in your game

  • Players: generate a batch, pick a full name that hints at your character's story, and use the nickname at the table so nobody has to say the whole thing every round.
  • Dungeon Masters: the 20-name batch fills a tabaxi trading caravan or a clan of wandering storytellers in seconds — each name already carries a hook.
  • Writers: a tabaxi's name is a tiny piece of flash fiction. Let the name suggest the backstory: why "Two Dry Cloaks"? Who gave them that name, and when?

How this generator builds names

Full names are assembled from poetic sentence patterns — an adjective and a noun, a thing set in a place, a number and an object, an action and its subject — drawn from nature-themed word pools in the tabaxi style. The nickname is pulled from a strong word in the full name, and clan names use a separate evocative pattern. Thousands of combinations, generated in your browser; nothing is stored.
